What Is Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)?
Deep Vein Thrombosis is a condition where a blood clot develops in a deep vein, usually in the calf or thigh. While DVT itself can cause pain and swelling, the biggest risk occurs when part of the clot breaks away and travels to the lungs.
This complication is called a pulmonary embolism (PE) and can lead to:
•	Sudden breathing difficulties
•	Chest pain
•	Reduced oxygen levels
•	Collapse or even death
Because DVT can become dangerous very quickly, immediate diagnosis is essential. This is why doctors often recommend urgent vascular ultrasound imaging when symptoms appear.

Why Early DVT Diagnosis Is So Important
DVT symptoms can sometimes appear gradually or even develop silently. Delaying medical attention increases the risk of serious complications.
An ultrasound scan is considered the gold standard imaging test for detecting DVT because it allows doctors to visualise blood flow and identify clots in the veins in real time.
Early diagnosis helps doctors:
•	Confirm or rule out DVT quickly
•	Start blood-thinning treatment if required
•	Prevent pulmonary embolism
•	Reduce the risk of long-term vein damage

How a DVT Ultrasound Works
A DVT ultrasound is a non-invasive scan that uses sound waves to examine blood flow inside the veins.
During the procedure:
•	A gel is applied to the skin over the affected leg
•	A handheld ultrasound probe is moved over the area
•	Images of the veins appear in real time on a monitor
•	The sonographer checks for blockages or clots
•	The scan is painless, radiation-free, and usually completed within 20–30 minutes.
Because results are available quickly, doctors can begin treatment immediately if a clot is detected.

Who Should Consider a DVT Ultrasound?
Your doctor may recommend a DVT ultrasound if you:
•	Have sudden swelling in one leg
•	Experience unexplained calf pain
•	Recently had surgery or prolonged bed rest
•	Have a history of blood clots
•	Recently travelled long distances
•	Are you pregnant or postpartum
Have certain medical conditions affecting blood clotting
People with risk factors should never ignore symptoms, as early imaging can prevent serious complications.

What Happens After the Scan?
Once the ultrasound is completed, the images are reviewed by a radiologist and sent to your referring doctor.
If DVT is confirmed, treatment may include:
•	Blood-thinning medication
•	Compression stockings
•	Monitoring and follow-up care
•	Hospital referral in severe cases
Early treatment greatly reduces the risk of pulmonary embolism and other complications.
